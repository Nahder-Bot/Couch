## 1. Market Size

### Streaming household baseline (US-focused)
- **~131M US households total** (Census 2024). **~39% include children under 18** → roughly **51M US family households with kids**. [estimate derived from Census] ([Census 2024](https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2024/11/family-households.html))
- **88–90% of US households** pay for at least one streaming service. ([MNTN Research](https://research.mountain.com/insights/89-of-u-s-households-pay-for-at-least-one-streaming-service/))
- **Avg ~4 active streaming apps per household**; Deloitte reports ~4 subs, some surveys go higher (~4.4–6). ([MNTN](https://research.mountain.com/trends/the-average-ctv-household-actively-uses-around-4-streaming-apps/), [Adwave Q1 2025](https://adwave.com/resources/average-streaming-subscriptions))
- **Avg monthly spend:** $61–$70/household. ([MNTN blog](https://mountain.com/blog/average-number-of-streaming-services/))
- **Families with kids stream ~35% more** than non-kid households and are the strongest co-viewing segment. ([TV Tech](https://www.tvtechnology.com/news/family-co-viewing-surges-among-streaming-audiences))

### Addressable target (rough TAM)
- **Primary ICP:** US multi-person households with kids, 2+ streaming services → **~45M households** [estimate: 51M kid-households × ~88% streaming penetration]
- **Extended ICP:** all multi-person streaming households (couples, roommates, adult families) → **~90–100M US households**. [estimate]
- **English-speaking TAM (US + UK + AU + CA):** add ~20–25M kid-households. [estimate]
- At a $4.99/mo family plan with even **0.5% penetration of the primary ICP** → **~225K paying households × $60/yr ≈ $13.5M ARR**. Not a unicorn, but a credible lifestyle-SaaS outcome.

### Co-viewing is growing, not shrinking
- **86% of families plan to watch MORE content together** — co-viewing is explicitly *not* a pandemic artifact. ([TV Tech](https://www.tvtechnology.com/news/family-co-viewing-surges-among-streaming-audiences))
- **94% of parents report increased co-viewing** in the past year.
- **95% of households with kids co-view regularly**; >50% of all streaming time is co-viewed. ([MNTN Research](https://research.mountain.com/insights/better-together-an-exploration-of-co-viewing-on-ctv/))

## 2. The "Decision Fatigue" Value Prop — strongest data point

This is Couch's single most defensible hook:

- **Nielsen (2023):** viewers spend **~10.5 minutes** picking what to watch; up from **7m 24s in 2019**. ([Deadline coverage](https://deadline.com/2019/07/streaming-overload-netflix-nielsen-report-average-viewer-takes-7-minutes-to-pick-what-to-watch-1202640213/))
- **Reelgood/Learndipity:** Netflix users spend ~**18 min** deciding. ([IndieWire](https://www.indiewire.com/news/general-news/netflix-decide-watch-studies-1201708634/))
- **1 in 5 (20%) viewers** abandon a session entirely because they can't decide. ([StreamTV Insider](https://www.streamtvinsider.com/video/choice-fatigue-leads-20-viewers-ditch-their-tv-session))
- Academic framing exists as **"Netflix Syndrome"** — choice deferral linked to measurable stress. ([AJPOR](https://www.ajpor.org/article/129993-why-does-netflix-syndrome-occur-a-study-on-the-effect-of-content-choice-deferral-on-stress))

**Strongest single datapoint for pitch decks:** *"The average family spends 10+ minutes deciding what to watch — and 1 in 5 gives up entirely. Couch ends the debate in under 60 seconds."*

## 3. Competitive Positioning Map

| Player | Owns | Audience | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Letterboxd** (26M users) | Serious cinephile *solo* logging/reviewing | <35, film nerds | Not built for families; zero co-decision UX ([Deadline](https://deadline.com/2025/01/letterboxd-indie-films-members-surge-in-2024-favorite-films-1236251217/)) |
| **JustWatch** | Cross-service search ("where can I stream X") | Already-decided viewers | Answers *where*, not *what* or *together* |
| **Plex** | Personal media library / self-hosted | Tech-inclined men 25–45 | Not a decision tool |
| **Teleparty / Scener / Kast** | Remote synchronous watch parties | LDR couples, friend groups | Assumes you've already decided + are apart |
| **Reelgood / TV Time / Trakt** | Watchlist aggregation / tracking | Solo power-users | Individual-focused; no household coordination |
| **Streaming platform's own "For You"** | Platform-siloed recs | Passive viewers | Siloed to one service; ignores household taste blend |

### The unclaimed quadrant
Map the two axes: **Solo ↔ Group** and **Decide ↔ Watch/Track**. Every incumbent clusters in the Solo/Track corner (Letterboxd, Trakt) or the Group/Watch corner (Teleparty). **The "Group × Decide" quadrant — the pre-play coordination layer for households that are already in the same room — is empty.**

That's Couch's moat: **the decision layer for households who will watch together, right now.**

## 4. Key Differentiators

1. **Household-first, not individual-first.** Letterboxd is "me and my taste." Couch is "us and our taste tonight."
2. **Pre-play, not mid-play.** Teleparty starts when you press play. Couch ends before you press play.
3. **Same-room, not long-distance.** Watch-party tools solve distance. Couch solves decision paralysis in the living room.
4. **Cross-service agnostic.** Not locked into a single streamer's catalogue.
5. **Kid-aware by design.** Age-appropriate filters baked in — a segment Letterboxd/Plex actively ignore.

### "Solo but social" vs strict group — signal check
There is a real signal that **asynchronous sharing > synchronous coordination** at scale: Letterboxd's explosive growth (1.8M → 26M in ~6 years) is pure async social. Teleparty and Scener have not scaled comparably. **Implication for Couch:** the *coordination* use case is more defensible (less competition, clearer pain) but the *ambient social* layer (friends' watchlists, "what did my sister love this month") is where viral growth likely lives. Build the decision core; layer ambient social for growth.

## 5. Pricing Benchmarks

- **2025 global medians (subscription apps):** $12.99/mo, $38.42/yr. Utility category tends higher on annual. ([Adapty State of In-App Subs 2026](https://adapty.io/state-of-in-app-subscriptions/))
- **US monthly avg ~$15.20;** Europe ~$13.30. ([Adapty](https://adapty.io/state-of-in-app-subscriptions/))
- **Teleparty charges $3.99–$6.99/mo** — a useful direct competitor anchor. ([Teleparty App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/teleparty-watch-party/id1620026075))
- **Family-plan tier** is a standard premium upsell pattern (meditation apps, etc.).

### Recommended pricing frame for Couch
- **Free:** 1 household, basic voting, 2–3 services
- **Family $4.99/mo or $39/yr:** unlimited services, watchlist history, kid profiles, extended family (grandparents) invites
- **Lifetime $49–$79:** strong anchor for an indie/family audience that hates recurring charges
- Anchoring below Teleparty's top tier ($6.99) signals accessibility; annual at $39 beats the global median and is defensible given the "replaces 10 min/night of friction" value story.

## 6. Recommended Tagline Options

Ranked, with the positioning bet each represents:

1. **"End the 'what should we watch?' debate."** — Directly names the pain. Universal.
2. **"The decision layer for family movie night."** — Category-defining; best for investor/press framing.
3. **"Pick something. Together. In under a minute."** — Emphasizes speed + the group benefit.
4. **"Couch: where your household agrees on what to watch."** — Household-first; good for App Store.
5. **"Stop scrolling. Start watching."** — Shortest, most punchy; weaker on the *together* angle.

**Top pick:** #1 for marketing + #2 for deck/press. They work as a pair.
