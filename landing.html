Why we built it

Blended family. Five kids. Two of them are in the basement halfway through one show. Three more are in three different rooms watching three different things. And the parents still want to put on something together at the end of the night.

"What are you watching?" became a daily logistics meeting. Who started what with whom. Who fell asleep on episode 4. Who's free tonight. What everyone could actually agree on if they tried.

Every household runs a smaller version of this. Couples who can't remember if they finished the show. Friends across time zones trying to start a series in sync. Anyone who's said "wait, don't spoil it."

Couch is the answer to "what are we watching tonight?" — and the bigger question underneath: who, with who, and where did we leave off.

Turn the argument into a ritual

Five people, three streaming services, two opinions on tone, one person already half-asleep. "What do you want to watch?" isn't a preference question — it's a coordination puzzle. Couch gives it two answers: vote together when nobody's decided, or drop a pick when one person already has one. Either way, tonight gets decided in 30 seconds, not 30 minutes.

Two ways to couch

THE SPIN

Everyone's home. Nobody's picked.

  1. Join the couch. Share a code. Everyone shows up.
  2. Everyone votes. Yes / maybe / no on the night's lineup. Takes 90 seconds.
  3. Spin decides. One spin, fair pick. Hate it? Veto. The wheel goes again.

THE NOMINATION

One person has a pick. Others decide in.

  1. Drop your pick. "I'm starting Blade Runner at 9pm."
  2. Everyone RSVPs. In / maybe / can't. See who's joining in real time.
  3. Couch calls it. Majority turns it into a watchparty. Everyone gets a heads-up when it's starting.

Both paths land the same place: everyone on the couch at the same time, watching the same thing.
